Module 7

Lead Through AI Incidents

AI incidents rarely begin with failure. They begin with something that doesn’t sit right — before all the facts are known. This module isn’t about policy, prevention or perfect governance. It’s about how you respond in that moment: calm, structured, accountable, and proportionate.

You cannot respond well until you understand what kind of failure you are dealing with.
→Recognise what counts as an AI incident or near miss
→Diagnose the type of failure before acting
→Take proportionate first moves that reduce repeat risk
→Stay visibly accountable without over-reacting

Module overview

When something doesn’t sit right

It might be a confident AI report that conflicts with your lived experience, an output that feels thin but is already shaping discussion, or a quiet concern raised by a team member. At that point you don’t yet know how serious it is — but accountability already exists, and how you respond now shapes what your team does next.

Leaders are judged not just on outcomes, but on how they respond when things go wrong. This module is about that moment: not diagnosing perfectly, but responding well.

The first leadership moment is when doubt appears. If leaders wait for dramatic failure, learning arrives too late — most harm comes from the quiet failures, not the dramatic ones.

Anchor scenarioThe ban that backfired

Your first leadership moment

Situation

After an AI-assisted report contained errors, a leader restricted the AI tool across the team. The tool had been used legitimately to draft and synthesise internal material.

What happened next

The team still needed the capability. Use didn’t stop — it moved to personal accounts, external tools, and informal workarounds. Visibility reduced. Risk increased.

When something goes wrong, what’s your instinct?

Default to broad restriction
Restrict or ban the tool across the team to be safe.
Contain the specific failure mode
Pause the exact step that failed; keep the rest working and visible.
A ban is not a response. It is a displacement.

Broad restriction doesn’t remove the need — it pushes use into the shadows, where you can’t see it. Containing the specific failure mode reduces risk while keeping the capability visible and accountable. Hold that instinct: you’ll return to it in the Response Console below.

Concept

What counts as an AI incident

Most AI failures don’t arrive looking like a disaster. They arrive looking like a slightly odd result — a report that doesn’t quite add up, an output a team member accepted because the AI sounded confident. An AI incident is any situation where AI use contributed to an outcome that was harmful, incorrect, or significantly outside intent — including near misses. Which of these qualify? Click each.

ISACA / MIT AI Incident Database (2025): the incidents causing the most organisational harm weren’t dramatic system failures — they were the quiet ones: a hiring system no one scrutinised, a chatbot giving confident wrong advice, a workflow that drifted until the human check was a formality. Individually they looked isolated. As a pattern, they were familiar, often predictable, and frequently avoidable.

Diagnose the failure before you act

When something goes wrong, the instinctive question is “what did the AI do?” The more useful one is “where did this failure actually originate?” Read the scenario and pick the most likely failure type.

Scenario — customer data across systems

An organisation used AI to work with customer data held across multiple systems. The use case was legitimate, the tool appropriate, the people acting in good faith. The AI began producing outputs that led to customers being contacted or treated as someone they were not — wrong identity, wrong context, wrong interaction. The immediate instinct was to look at the AI.

Leader script — “We’re going to separate facts, causes, and accountability. We can be firm on standards without turning this into blame theatre. If you have context that helps us understand what happened, bring it forward now — no surprises.”
Good-faith judgementIncomplete information, acting in good faith — respond with learning.
Knowingly bypassing a controlA different conversation — about standards.
Unsafe path was easiestA system-design problem — the workflow made it likely.

Prevent governance drift

Of all failure modes, governance drift most consistently goes unnoticed until it produces a significant outcome. It doesn’t start with a decision to stop following the process — a good process just erodes quietly.

Case — the process that stayed on paper

A team introduced an AI-assisted process for weekly reporting narratives, with a mandatory human review step. For several weeks the review was thorough. Then timelines tightened, the AI outputs kept looking reasonable, and the reviewer who owned the step moved roles. Nobody reassigned it. The step stayed on the process document — but stopped happening. Weeks later, unusual external conditions produced results the AI narrative explained plausibly but entirely wrongly. The report went out. Decisions were made. No individual had done anything obviously wrong.

Four small factors caused the drift — none significant alone. Mark the one most present in your environment.

The corrective action is not a new AI rule. Reassign ownership of the review step, clarify exactly what it must catch, and add a way to verify it’s happening. Then prevent recurrence with the guardrails you designed in Module 6.

The first 48 hours — calm accountability

Scenario

An AI-assisted report your team produced last week has been circulated to a senior stakeholder. A team member has just told you a key data input was incomplete and the conclusions may be unreliable. The stakeholder hasn’t acted on it yet.

✓ Saved

Your biggest task isn’t to be certain — it’s to be visible, calm and credible while the facts emerge. When leaders go quiet to “get the full picture,” a vacuum forms and the narrative forms without you. Credibility comes from saying three things early and consistently:

Signal three non-negotiables
  • We act proportionately, not theatrically — pause the specific risk point, not the whole tool.
  • We protect honest disclosure — concerns raised early are handled as learning, not scapegoating.
  • We preserve the trail — capture what happened before changes are made, so we can learn.
What to avoid in the first 48 hours
  • Quietly “fixing” it and moving on.
  • Waiting for certainty before speaking.
  • Resolving it privately before the right functions have visibility.
A practical leader prompt: “We’ve paused this specific output while we check the source data. We are not banning the tool, but we are not relying on this report until the issue is understood.”

The Response Console — Contain · Escalate · Correct

Once you have initial facts and a working diagnosis, use this three-stage model. The stages can overlap, but each has a different purpose. Work the case below through it.

Case — the update that kept moving

A team used AI to generate weekly operational updates for senior leaders. The tool was approved for drafting and pattern recognition, but the final update was meant to be reviewed by a manager before it informed planning. Over time the update moved faster; the outputs usually looked credible, and the review became lighter. In one cycle the AI-generated update overstated likely demand in one part of the business and understated risk in another. It was shared internally and was about to inform a resource-allocation decision. A team member noticed before the decision was finalised and raised it.

1 · Contain

Stop the spread

A temporary, targeted pause at the specific failure point — preserve evidence.

2 · Escalate

Visibility, not panic

Get the right roles visibility early — based on risk type, not embarrassment.

3 · Correct

Change the conditions

Fix the root cause and add a way to detect if the fix is working.

Key takeaways

Incidents begin with doubt, not disaster. The first leadership moment is when something doesn’t sit right.
Diagnose before you act. Model, data or governance — the right fix depends entirely on which.
A ban is a displacement. Contain the specific failure; don’t drive use underground with blanket restriction.
Avoid the double error. Overcorrecting and losing confidence both feel decisive but neither fixes the cause.
First 48 hours = calm accountability. Say what you know, what you don’t, and what you’re doing next.
Correct the conditions. Real fixes match the failure type, name an owner, and add detection.
